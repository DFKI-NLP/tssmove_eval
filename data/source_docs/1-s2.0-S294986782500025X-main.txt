**1. Introduction**



A B S T R A C T


The integration of Global Navigation Satellite System (GNSS) technology into railway train control systems is a
crucial step toward achieving the vision of a digital railway. Traditional train control systems undergo extensive
in-house tests and prolonged field tests for certification and approval before operational deployment, leading to
high costs, delays, and operational disruptions. This paper introduces a GNSS-based train control localization
framework which eliminates the need for on-site testing by leveraging train movement dynamics and 3D en­
vironment modeling to create a zero on-site testing platform. The proposed framework simulates train move­
ment and the surrounding 3D environment using collected railway line location data and environmental attri­
butes to generate realistic multipath signals and obscuration effects. This approach enables comprehensive
laboratory-based case studies for train localization, reducing the huge amount test of needed for physical field
trials. The framework is established in house, using the data collected at the Test Base of China Academy of
Railway Sciences (Circular Railway). Results from the open area and cutting environment tests demonstrate high
localization accuracy repeatability within the simulated environment, validating the feasibility and effectiveness
of zero on-site testing for GNSS-based train control systems. This research highlights the potential of GNSS
simulation platforms in enhancing cost efficiency, operational safety, and accuracy for future digital railways.



Global Navigation Satellite Systems (GNSSs) have seen vast applications
in the transportation domain. The integration of Global Positioning System
(GPS) and Beidou Navigation Satellite System (BDS) in railway is efficient
for modernizing the railway network, GNSS is increasingly becoming a
fundamental pillar not only for non-safety applications, but also for im­
proved maintenance, automated train operation, and safety- related appli­
cations, including the Next Generation of Train Control (NGTC) and re­
novation of the train on-board equipment, all these will increase the railway
system capacity, efficiency and safety of all railways [1].
To apply GNSS in railway train operation, the GNSS performance testing
and verification is the essential procedure for the system to be adopted and
further listed as mandatory in the system design and development. The
certification of the GNSS-based train localization systems is also necessary to
provide concrete validation or testing application services to carry out



simulation tests with evaluation of the platform and environmental condi­
tion simulations [2]. Beidou has a set of technical and regulatory standards,
including the BDS Performance Standard, BDS Signal Interface Control
Document (ICD), and technical requirements for user equipment. The BDS
Signal In Space (SIS) and service performance characteristics include ac­
curacy, availability, compatibility, and interoperability [3]. GPS has also
published standard positioning service performance standards to guide the
user for civil uses worldwide, the performance metrics are GPS SIS accuracy,
continuity, and availability [4]. So both BDS and GPS are focusing on ac­
curacy as the foundation for the GNSS performance [5]. The performance
needs to be assessed and as the performances are all credited to the
equipment, the equipment itself needs to be certified. The certification
procedure, it is governed by various international and regional standards,
such as the accuracy standards by BDS and GPS, the test and simulation
standard procedures, the simulation must meet possible (as sensible to the
GNSS) to make the test results plausible. Railway GNSS related testing is

**2. Problems description**


Since the benefits of zero on-site testing have been illustrated, the
state-of-the-art for simulation of GNSS has also been demonstrated, but
there are still scientific problems and challenges to be addressed for
implementing zero on-site testing. The zero on-site testing procedure
shall be composed of the procedure starting with the test control and
management, then the train movement and simulation. With the si­
mulated train trajectory similar to Fig. 1, the GNSS signal simulation
and the modeled 3D environment together can generate the GNSS
signal by the GNSS simulator, the simulated GNSS signal propagation
needs to be modeled in the simulator to evaluate the Non-Line-Of-Sight
(NLOS) multipath effects estimations [14]. With the generated GNSS
signal, the signal can either be coupled directly into the device under
test through SMA or TNC interfaces or using the Over-The-Air (OTA)
testing, the GNSS signal propagation in the environment can be shown
directly using the 3D ray tracing theories and methods [13,15]. The
whole procedure can be shown in Fig. 3.
The main problem and challenge for the test can be illustrated as
follows.


1) Train movement simulation: the train movement needs to be si­

mulated as identical to the train operation, since when the train
operates the GNSS receiver mounted on the train should receive the
GNSS signal, and the train moving along the track needs to have the
right occupation which needs to be demonstrated on the railway
simulation platform. Thus, the train maneuver dynamics should be
modeled in accordance with the correct train type. An accurate train
dynamics model is fundamental to train movement algorithms.



2) GNSS signal propagation: with the train movement, the GNSS signal
propagation in the real world can be affected by the environment
along the railway track. As the optimized signal to be propagated is
the Line-Of-Sight (LOS) signal, due to the obstacles along the track,
the GNSS signal might be reflected, refracted, and blocked. Thus,
environmental modelling is the basis for simulation.


There are still many challenges in addressing scientific problems.
The main problems to be discussed in this paper is the environmental
modelling forming the ray-tracing procedure, and the train movement
that integrates the environment and train location to generate the ap­
propriate GNSS signal for the zero on-site testing.


**3. Key technologies in zero on-site testing of GNSS**


_3.1. GNSS testing standard and regulations_


As illustrated in the introduction part, the public service performance
evaluation of GNSS covers two key aspects: SIS performance and system
service performance. As the BeiDou Navigation Satellite System Open
Service Performance Standard (BDS-OS-PS-3.0) defines, the BDS service
performance should cover 5 core indicators: positioning accuracy, velocity
measurement accuracy, timing accuracy, PDOP availability, and positioning
service availability, as shown in Fig. 4. In terms of positioning accuracy, the
specification clarifies the statistical standard of geographical location mea­
surement error and specifies the allowable error range under different
geographical areas and environmental conditions. The evaluation of speed
measurement accuracy is concerned with the accuracy of the user equip­
ment to measure the motion speed, which is used in dynamic scenarios,
such as transportation and railway applications. Timing accuracy is aimed
at high-precision time synchronization services, which are critical cap­
abilities in fields such as communications, energy, and scientific experi­
ments. The availability of PDOP is an important index to evaluate the effect
of geometric configuration on positioning accuracy and to measure the
degree of support of satellite layout to the stability of positioning solution.
Location service availability measures the ability to provide continuous and
reliable service at a given time and area from the perspective of the overall
functionality of the system.
In addition, the standard also specifies the measurement methods of this
performance, including the evaluation environment, data collection
method, calculation method, and evaluation cycle specific requirements.
For example, positioning accuracy and velocity measurement accuracy are
usually obtained by field testing in different geographical areas and oper­
ating scenarios, while timing accuracy needs to be compared with a highprecision reference time source. For PDOP and service availability, the si­
mulation platform is often used for spatial geometry layout analysis. Several
projects of Shift2Rail, such as Gate4Rail and Sensor4Rail, have validated the
potential of GNSS in train monitoring, real-time trajectory planning and
train spacing optimization. These projects combined simulation and field
tests to demonstrate the robustness of GNSS in multi-interference scenarios
and its ability to optimize positioning accuracy.

the 1D error as east and north. Besides, the error can be estimated by

_3.2. 3D environment reconstruction and ray-tracing_



The core of the 3D simulation platform is the environment 3D modeling
and the satellite signal channel modeling through ray tracing. As a method
of channel modeling, ray tracing can predict signal parameters by simu­
lating electromagnetic wave signal emission and propagation process. GNSS
signal, as a typical electromagnetic wave signal, can effectively determine
the channel parameters of LOS signal and NLOS signal through raytracing.
As shown in Fig. 6, the sender (TX) emits the signal, the trajectory is re­
flected by the surrounding object, the receiver (RX) signal has second-orderreflection, thus as the NLOS signal.
In the defined environment, the environmental data of the target sce­
nario is mapped using LiDAR, 3D modeling is conducted by the method of
UAV oblique photography. Since the environmental parameters, satellite
ephemeris and trajectory simulation time are pre-defined conditions or in­
formation, the channel model of the satellite signal is a deterministic
channel, in which the electromagnetic wave propagation theory can be used
to derive the electromagnetic wave channel. This experiment focuses on the
multipath effect in satellite signal propagation, so it focuses on the direct
and reflection of electromagnetic waves.

_4.2. Zero on-site testing platform_


The zero on-site testing platform integrates the train dynamics and
environment generation module, signal generation module, test man­
agement module, and evaluation module to form the zero on-site
testing on GNSS for the Train Control platform, as shown in Fig. 13.



The test management module controls the test process with the test
case and test sequence for the simulator control; the train dynamics and
environment generation module generates the train movement, and the
train movement is converted from mileage to the WGS-84 latitude and
longitude information using the digital track map; then the signal
generation module generates the BDS/GPS signal together with the
multipath signal, all the signals are generated into the device under test
in the anechoic chamber; finally, the evaluation module performs the
data management and using the evaluation algorithm to go through the
evaluation and display the 3D ray-tracing process. The hardware of the
platform is shown in Fig. 14.

**5. Conclusion**


This paper introduces a zero on-site testing platform on GNSS for
train control towards digital railways, the platform utilizes the GNSS RF
signal to be broadcasted through the multiplier of both GNSS signal and
multipath signal, which enables the receiver to operate in an environ­
ment where the signal reception is identical to the physical world. The
proposed methods in this paper train dynamic modeling, 3D environ­
ment modeling with digital track map, and the ray-tracing techniques.
With the proposed method and the setup of the zero on-site platform,
the tests on open area and cutting environment are tested, and the test
results demonstrate the zero on-site ability of the platform.
Further, repeatability and reproducibility need to be performed for
the accreditation of the platform for the adoption of the certification
process for the GNSS in railway applications. By reducing the cost and
risks in field testing, zero on-site testing emerges as a highly advanta­
geous method for future railway system validation and deployment.
