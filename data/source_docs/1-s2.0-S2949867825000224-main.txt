**1. Introduction**



A B S T R A C T


The adoption of 5G for Railways (5G-R) is expanding, particularly in high-speed trains, due to the benefits
offered by 5G technology. High-speed trains must provide seamless connectivity and Quality of Service (QoS) to
ensure passengers have a satisfactory experience throughout their journey. Installing base stations along urban
environments can improve coverage but can dramatically reduce the experience of users due to interference. In
particular, when a user with a mobile phone is a passenger in a high speed train traversing between urban
centres, the coverage and the 5G resources in general need to be adequate not to diminish her experience of the
service. The utilization of macro, pico, and femto cells may optimize the utilization of 5G resources. In this
paper, a Genetic Algorithm (GA)-based approach to address the challenges of 5G network planning for 5G-R
services is presented. The network is divided into three cell types, macro, pico, and femto cells—and the opti­
mization process is designed to achieve a balance between key objectives: providing comprehensive coverage,
minimizing interference, and maximizing energy efficiency. The study focuses on environments with high user
density, such as high-speed trains, where reliable and high-quality connectivity is critical. Through simulations,
the effectiveness of the GA-driven framework in optimizing coverage and performance in such scenarios is
demonstrated. The algorithm is compared with the Particle Swarm Optimisation (PSO) and the Simulated
Annealing (SA) methods and interesting insights emerged. The GA offers a strong balance between coverage and
efficiency, achieving significantly higher coverage than PSO while maintaining competitive energy efficiency
and interference levels. Its steady fitness improvement and adaptability make it well-suited for scenarios where
wide coverage is a priority alongside acceptable performance trade-offs.

mobile users to either a macro, pico, or femto base station. This choice
is crucial, as it directly influences the energy demands of each type of
base station. Careful management of which base station serves a given
user can significantly optimize the energy efficiency of the overall
network.
Genetic Algorithms (GAs) are widely used in a variety of applica­
tions [8]. GAs offer significant benefits in solving real-world problems,
particularly those involving complex, nonlinear, and multi-dimensional
search spaces. Inspired by the principles of natural selection, GAs excel
at finding optimal or near-optimal solutions by exploring vast solution
spaces efficiently. One of the key advantages is their ability to handle
problems where traditional optimization methods struggle, such as
those with multiple local optima, discontinuous spaces, or no clear
gradient to follow. By evolving candidate solutions through mutation,
crossover, and selection, GAs can adapt to changing environments,
making them especially useful for dynamic or real-time problem-sol­
ving. Their stochastic nature ensures diversity in solutions, often
leading to more robust and innovative outcomes that traditional algo­
rithms may overlook.
In this paper, we utilize a basic GA to tackle the challenges of 5G
network planning to target high speed train user experience. The



network is divided into three cell types: macro, pico, and femto cells.
Our optimization process aims to balance multiple key objective­

imizing energy efficiency. The focus is on servicing environments where
numerous users coexist, particularly in high-speed trains that demand
reliable, high-quality connectivity. Through a series of simulations, we
uncover valuable insights into the effectiveness of our approach in
addressing these complex network planning challenges. These findings
demonstrate the potential of using a GA-based framework to adapt to
dynamic, high-demand environments like high-speed transit systems.


**2. Related work**


The is a number of research works that address the 5G network
planning using numerous different techniques.
In Ref. [9], the authors explore the use of Machine Learning (ML) to
approximate a complex 5G path loss model, significantly reducing
calculation time, and making it suitable for integration into a GA-based
network planning tool. The ML model is trained using data from two
buildings and validated on three additional buildings, achieving a mean
absolute error of less than 3 dB. The new approach demonstrates the
ability to determine a wireless network deployment solution with the
same or fewer access points while maintaining the required coverage
for at least 99.4 % of receiver locations—15 times faster than tradi­
tional methods. Unlike heuristic approaches, this method also accom­
modates additional design considerations, such as maximizing average
received power across the building or minimizing radio frequency ex­
posure in specific rooms.
Ref. [10] distinguishes between existing research that emphasizes to
the use of deterministic algorithms or meta-heuristic algorithms tar­
geting a single objective and GAs. In particular, this paper harnesses the
power of GAs, specifically a Multi-Objective Genetic Algorithm
(MOGA), to address multiple objectives simultaneously. The key ob­
jectives include minimizing cost and interference while maximizing
user coverage in network deployment scenarios. The experimental re­
sults demonstrate that the use of a GA, particularly with stochastic
universal selection, is highly effective in balancing these competing
goals. The approach not only meets the objectives but also converges to
a near-optimal solution efficiently within a relatively short time frame.
This showcases the ability of MOGAs to handle complex, multi-faceted
optimization problems that involve trade-offs between multiple criteria,
offering a flexible and robust solution for real-world network planning
challenges. The Particle Swarm Optimisation (PSO) and the Simulated
Annealing (SA) have been employed as methods that can compete with
the proposed GA solution.
The work in Ref. [11] examines the planning process for a 5G radio
access network utilizing mmWave Micro Remote Radio Units (mRRUs)
in conjunction with sub-6 GHz Macro Remote Radio Units (MRRUs).
Their approach leverages accurate channel models, link budgets, and
the specific characteristics of Urban Macro-cells (UMas) and Urban
Micro-cells (UMis) to carefully formulate an optimization problem for
5G network planning. The goal is to determine the minimum number
and optimal locations of MRRUs and mRRUs required to meet coverage
and user traffic demand constraints within a defined geographical area.
To address this, we propose a two-step process: first, a low-complexity
meta-heuristic algorithm is used to optimize RRU locations, followed by
an iterative elimination method to remove redundant cells. To assess
the effectiveness of this approach, we perform a comparative analysis
using accelerated particle swarm optimization and SA techniques.
The research in Ref. [12] addresses the problem of determining the
optimal set of cells to turn off in a network, which is NP-hard. In this
study, the authors utilize a heuristic algorithm which focuses on
switching off underutilized base station components while ensuring
user demands are met. We introduce a new Energy-Saving (ES)

**3. Our methodology and system**


The planning process for a 5G network involves the deployment of
macro, pico, and femto cells to achieve optimal coverage, minimize
costs, and reduce interference in connection between urban environ­
ments high speed trains operate. The objective is to ensure that all user
locations receive adequate signal strength while meeting varying traffic
demands. This requires a careful balance between the number of cells
deployed and their respective locations. This will ensure the Quality of
Experience (QoE) of the train passengers in their activities with their
mobile phones. The reader can see a schematic of the approach in
Fig. 1. In Fig. 1, the reader can observe high speed trains going in
different directions with passengers using the 5G services. The different
types of cells are evident that aim to maximize the user experience by
performing appropriate planning. Note that the blue arrow denotes the
fact that the train operates within the cell it points to. The decision

variables in a way that satisfies coverage and user traffic demand

**4. Simulated experiment**


Simulations are run in Python using the following parameters. The po­
pulation size of the cells is set to 100. The objective is that the population size
will allow for more diversity in potential solutions, improving the chances of
finding an optimal configuration. The number of generations, which is the
total number of iterations the GA will run is set to 300. The crossover rate,
which is the probability that 2 % configurations will exchange genetic ma­
terial to produce offspring is set to 0.7. The mutation rate, which is the
probability that a mutation will occur in an individual during reproduction,
introduces random changes is set to 0.05. The stochastic universal sampling
was employed to select individuals from the population for reproduction.
The area is assumed to be 100 km [2 ] and the train speed is 250 km/h.
The chosen GA parameters—population size of 100, 300 genera­
tions, a crossover rate of 0.7, a mutation rate of 0.05, and stochastic
universal sampling can be considered as appropriate baseline values in
evolutionary algorithm literature. These settings are known to offer a
robust trade-off between exploration and exploitation across a range of
optimization problems. A population size of 100 and 300 generations
allows for sufficient diversity and evolutionary depth, while the cross­
over and mutation rates fall within empirically validated ranges (typi­
cally 0.6–0.9 for crossover and 0.01–0.1 for mutation). Stochastic uni­
versal sampling is used to maintain selection pressure while preserving



population diversity. These values offer a reliable starting point for
performance evaluation. However sensitivity analysis may be more
appropriate for fine-tuning the approach.

The PSO and SA have been utilized as a comparison to the GA that is
described in this paper. The authors compared the GA with the afore­
mentioned methods in terms of coverage, interference, best fitness, and
energy efficiency.
The PSO algorithm is a swarm-based optimization algorithm modeled on
the collective behavior of birds, where each solution (or “particle”) updates
its position by considering both its own best-known position and that of the
swarm, making it effective for solving complex, continuous optimization
problems such as those in 5G network planning. In contrast, SA is a prob­
abilistic search method that allows occasional acceptance of inferior solu­
tions to escape local optima, with this acceptance decreasing over time—an
approach particularly useful for navigating highly nonlinear and multimodal
problem spaces. Both methods are particularly well-suited for 5G-R network
optimization due to their ability to efficiently explore large, complex search
spaces and avoid premature convergence to suboptimal configurations.

**6. Conclusions and future work**


In this work, the issue of 5G network planning for maximizing user
experience in high speed trains moving along urban environments is ad­
dressed. The network needs to exhibit high coverage and small inter­
ference to be successful. The planning of the network aims directly to that.
A GA is employed to address the complexities of 5G network plan­
ning. By segmenting the network into macro, pico, and femto cells, the
optimization process was designed to meet key goals: achieving broad
coverage, reducing interference, and improving energy efficiency. The
primary focus was on high-demand environments, such as high-speed
trains, where numerous users require stable and high-quality con­
nectivity. Through extensive simulations, valuable insights were gained
into the effectiveness of our GA-based method as compared to the PSO
algorithm and SA in solving these challenges.
Despite not outperforming the PSO algorithm in all aspects, the GA
demonstrates distinct advantages that make it a valuable optimization
approach in wireless network design. Notably, GA achieves significantly
higher coverage (3660 km [2] ) than the PSO algorithm, which is critical for
ensuring broader service availability and user connectivity—especially in
dense or large-scale deployments. While the PSO algorithm favors energy
efficiency and minimal interference by reducing coverage, GA strategically
balances these trade-offs, delivering competitive energy efficiency
(39.68 J) and manageable interference (58.28 dB). Furthermore, GA shows
a steady improvement in fitness over generations, highlighting its ro­
bustness in navigating complex solution spaces. These qualities make GA
particularly suitable for applications where maximizing coverage without
severely compromising efficiency is essential, showcasing its strength in
multi-objective optimization scenarios.
For future work, the aim is to perform cell zooming to further op­
timize the planning procedure. Cell zooming is the zooming in and out
of base station cells with respect to their transmission power to reduce
interference further. The approach that will be followed by increasing
the number of colours to perform a game-theoretic graph coloring so­
