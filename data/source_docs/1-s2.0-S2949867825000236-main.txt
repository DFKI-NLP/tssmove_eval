**1. Introduction**



A B S T R A C T


Global Navigation Satellite System (GNSS)-based continuous and accurate train positioning is one of the key
technologies for advanced train operations such as train virtual coupling. However, GNSS-based train posi­
tioning faces significant challenges in real-world scenarios due to environmental complexities and signal in­
terferences. Considering this issue, this paper presents an approach for modeling and performance analysis of
GNSS-based train positioning systems using Colored Petri Nets (CPNs). By systematically modeling the GNSS
signal reception and processing process, the performance of the positioning system under various environment
scenarios is evaluated. The system model integrates three types of interference signals (i.e., Amplitude
Modulation (AM) signals, Frequency Modulation (FM) signals, and pulse signals) while incorporating environ­
mental factors such as terrain obstructions and tunnel shielding. Additionally, the Extended Kalman Filter (EKF)
algorithm is employed to process GNSS observation data, providing accurate train position estimations. The
simulation results demonstrate that signal interferences and complex environmental conditions significantly
affect the GNSS-based positioning accuracy. This study offers a comprehensive framework for evaluating the
performance of GNSS-based train positioning systems in different scenarios, highlighting critical factors that
influence positioning accuracy and stability.



With the increasing demand for operation safety and efficiency in
rail transportation, train virtual coupling has been proposed and be­
come a hot research topic in recent years. For virtual coupling, con­
tinuous and accurate train positioning is a cornerstone. Due to the
global coverage and high availability, Global Navigation Satellite
Systems (GNSSs) have been widely employed for train positioning [1].
However, GNSS-based positioning faces significant challenges in com­
plex operational environments such as mountainous regions and tun­
nels, where signal obstructions and interferences lead to accuracy de­
gradation.
Extensive researches have focused on the application of GNSS in
train positioning, specifically under idealized conditions with unin­
terrupted and unobstructed signal reception [2–4]. Existing methods,
such as Extended Kalman Filters (EKFs) and data fusion with inertial
sensors, have been demonstrated notable in improving positioning



accuracy [5,6]. However, these works lack a comprehensive perfor­
mance analysis of GNSS-based positioning in real-world operational
environments such as tunnels and mountainous terrains, where signal
interferences and obstructions occur frequently.
One of the critical challenges in applying GNSS-based train posi­
tioning is analyzing its performances under various operational condi­
tions including that GNSS signals are interfered with by interference
signals such as Amplitude Modulation (AM) signals, Frequency
Modulation (FM) signals, and pulse signals, or obstructed by mountains
and tunnels. Traditional approaches often fail to intuitively represent
the dynamic interactions of GNSS signals and the environmental effects
on positioning accuracy. To this end, this paper proposes a Colored
Petri Net (CPN)-based approach to analyze the performance of GNSSbased train positioning systems. CPNs provide a powerful framework to
describe the dynamic behaviors and concurrent processes of GNSS
signal reception, signal interferences, and environmental impacts of the
positioning systems. The objective of this study is to evaluate the

**2. Related works**


Over the past decades, GNSS-based systems have been extensively ap­
plied to train positioning due to their global coverage and high accuracy
under favorable conditions [1,7]. Despite these advantages, challenges such
as signal interferences and terrain-induced occlusions continue to pose
questions on high positioning accuracy and stability. Numerous studies have
explored techniques to mitigate the impacts of signal interferences on GNSSbased positioning accuracy [8–12]. For example, Ref. [9] investigates the
impact of continuous wave interference on GNSS receiver tracking perfor­
mance, examining the extent of its influence by varying data bit durations
or integration times. To address interference-related challenges, an inter­
ference energy detection method and a comprehensive orientation tech­
nology tailored for GNSS are introduced in Ref. [11]. Besides, a number of
researches have examined the impact of environmental factors along
railway lines on GNSS signal integrity. For instance, Ref. [13] explores
methods of scenario-based weighted measurement noise calculation and
fault detection combined with state recognition based on integrity evalua­
tion in autonomous train positioning systems. Some studies have achieved
high positioning accuracy in partially obstructed or challenging environ­
ments by fusing multi-sensor data [6,14,15].
In contrast to studies that mainly investigate GNSS signal inter­
ferences in isolation, this paper integrates environmental factors and
signal interferences into the formal model of the positioning system. By
utilizing model simulation, the proposed approach enables a quantita­
tive evaluation of the system’s performance. This method provides a
systematic analysis and verification of the interactions among signal
interferences, environmental conditions, and train positioning.
Due to the rigorous mathematical foundation, formal methods have
gained recognition as effective tools for system modeling and



verification. In this work, we adopt CPNs [16] as the formal modeling
framework to study the impact of signal interferences and environ­
mental factors on GNSS-based train positioning. Petri nets are formal
modeling languages that are well-known for systems characterized as
concurrent, synchronous, distributed, etc. Some application examples
adopting petri nets as the means of description for railway signaling
systems can be found in Refs. [17–22].


**3. System modeling with CPNs**


Fig. 1 illustrates the modeling framework of a GNSS-based train
positioning system comprising the following main parts: GNSS Re­
ceiver, Environment Scenarios, Interference Signals, GNSS Position
Solution, and Evaluation Module. The module GNSS Receiver selects
specific environment scenarios and receives GNSS signals. The
module Environment Scenarios is composed of three submodules:
Open Area Scenario, Mountain Occlusion Scenario, and Tunnel and
Exit Tunnel Scenario. These submodules simulate various real-world
conditions while generating GNSS signal interferences, i.e., GNSS
signals are interfered with by AM, FM, and pulse signals. In the
module GNSS Positioning Solution, the EKF algorithm is employed to
process interfered GNSS signals for estimating the train’s position.
The module Evaluation compares the calculated position with the
reference position and outputs the positioning error. Fig. 2 clearly
illustrates the hierarchical relationships between modules of the CPN
model, providing a structured view of their layered organization. The
declarations of the model are categorized and summarized in two

_3.2.1. Submodule Open Area_
Fig. 5 illustrates the CPN model designed to simulate the impacts of
GNSS signal interferences on the observation signals. This model in­
troduces three typical types of interference signals, i.e., AM, FM, and
pulse signals. Boolean variables are used to control the activation of
each interference type. This design ensures that the generation process
of each interference signal is both independent and easily manageable.
Additionally, it accurately reflects the characteristics of realistic signal
interference environments.



For the transition AM Interference, amplitude-modulated inter­
ference signals are generated by multiplying a low-frequency sinusoidal
envelope (1 Hz) with a high-frequency carrier wave centered at
1575.42 MHz, corresponding to the GNSS L1 band. This configuration
produces a time-varying envelope with a modulation depth of 0.5, ef­
fectively simulating realistic amplitude fluctuations observed in Radio
Frequency (RF) interference sources. A similar signal interference set­
ting is illustrated in Ref. [23]. Dynamic amplitude variations can sig­
nificantly degrade GNSS signal reception, particularly in high-noise or
contested environments.
The transition FM Interference simulates frequency-modulated
signal interference by generating two sinusoidal components with
symmetrical frequency deviations around a carrier frequency centered
at 1575.42 MHz, corresponding to the GNSS L1 band. These deviations
are sampled from a Gaussian distribution with a standard deviation of
75 kHz, emulating the spectral characteristics of typical FM broadcast
signals [24]. This approach captures the dynamic spectral spreading
commonly observed in real-world RF interference.
The transition Pulse Interference generates periodic pulse signals to si­
mulate sudden and intense bursts of RF energy. It samples two random
parameters: the pulse width _T_ p which represents the duration of each pulse,
and the pulse interval _Ti_ which defines the spacing between successive
pulses. A uniformly distributed amplitude between 1 and 5 simulates the
varying severity of the interference. At each time step, the model checks
whether the current time modulo the total pulse period _T_ p + _Ti_ falls within
the pulse’s active duration. If so, an interference signal of the specified
amplitude is emitted; otherwise, it remains zero. This periodic pulsed
structure reflects typical burst-like interference patterns observed in GNSS
environments [25], characterized by sharp, short-lived increases in signal
strength followed by rapid attenuation.
The input data enter the module through the input places Open Area
and GNSS Signal, representing the GNSS satellites and the initial en­
vironmental conditions. The place Bool Control and transition Mutex
manage the logic for introducing signal interference, with the code in
Mutex ensuring that the generation processes for different interference
types do not conflict. Each interference signal is integrated into the
GNSS data by firing the transition Interference. The GNSS data affected
by signal interferences proceed to the observation output stage con­
trolled by the place GNSS Number. The final results are outputted from
the output place GNSS Observation.

_3.2.3. Submodule Tunnel_
Fig. 7 illustrates the process of GNSS signal reception and error variation
for a train in a tunnel scenario. It focuses on the dynamic impact of the
tunnel environment on the satellite signal visibility and positioning accu­
racy. In this module, the places InTunnel, Just Out, and Out Tunnel re­
present the train’s current state, including three phases: inside the tunnel,
just outside the tunnel, and completely outside the tunnel.



Inside the tunnel, a train is shielded by the structure blocking
GNSS signal reception. During this phase, the place GNSS Signal
remains empty indicating the complete obstruction of satellite sig­
nals. When the train just exits the tunnel, the GNSS signals become
visible again. However, due to the complex environmental condi­
tions near the tunnel exit, such as multipath effects, signal reflec­
tions, and delays, the GNSS observation data contain significant er­
rors. This process is modeled by the code of the transition Tunnel
Scenario, which integrates the tunnel state from the place Tunnel
with visible satellite information. Over time, as the GNSS signal re­
ception stabilizes, the positioning errors gradually decrease. This
dynamic adjustment is governed by the place Time Counter. The
place GNSS Observation outputs the GNSS data affected by the tunnel
scenario, including both the high-error initial data and the stabilized
high-accuracy data. This module effectively captures the three-phase
impact of tunnel scenarios on GNSS signal reception: complete signal
loss inside the tunnel, significant errors immediately after exiting,
and eventual signal stabilization outside the tunnel.

**4. Simulation and analysis results**


To ensure the correctness of the CPN model, formal verification is
essential. Table 3 presents part of the standard state space report for the
developed model. The state space is complete, comprising 5365 mark­
ings and 6410 arcs. The Strongly Connected Component (SCC) graph
contains the same number of nodes and arcs, indicating the absence of
cycles in the state space. The verification was performed using five
representative input datasets, which cover all potential operational
paths of the system. This ensures that the generated state space fully
captures the range of behaviors that the model can exhibit under dif­
ferent input conditions. The home properties show that the initial
marking is not a home marking. According to the liveness properties,
the model includes 648 dead markings, but no dead or live transition
instances. The fairness properties confirm that there are no infinite



occurrence sequences. These findings together demonstrate the model’s
soundness, boundedness, and finite execution. The existence of dead
markings suggests that certain process branches reach terminal states
where no further transitions are possible. This behavior is consistent
with the expected functioning of the train positioning system, where a
positioning cycle may conclude or the process may terminate early due
to data filtering or error detection.
To simulate train positioning, loops are added to the model. Each
unit of time in the model corresponds to one second in the real world. A
simulation running for 600-time units represents ten minutes of station
operation. To facilitate data analysis, a breakpoint monitor is set to halt
the simulation when the model time exceeds 600-time units.
The dataset used for the simulation is based on a segment of the
actual Jing–Shen high-speed railway. Track coordinates are derived
from field measurements, while satellite ephemeris data are obtained
from standard RINEX broadcast ephemeris files. At each observation
epoch along the track, visible satellites and their corresponding signal
parameters are calculated. Specifically, satellite positions are de­
termined using broadcast ephemeris data through standard GNSS or­
bital propagation models. True pseudoranges are computed based on
the geometric distance between the satellite and the receiver, assuming
ideal signal propagation without any observation errors. Azimuth and
elevation angles are computed by transforming the satellite coordinates
into the local horizon coordinate system relative to the receiver’s po­
sition. These computations adhere to established GNSS observation

Under interference-free conditions, the positioning system demon­
strates high accuracy and stability, with a mean error of 1.0279 m and a
standard deviation of 0.0552 m. However, under signal interference
conditions, the system’s performance degrades significantly. The AM
signal interference increases the mean error to 4.9484 m and the stan­
dard deviation to 4.0833 m, with the elevation direction showing the
most significant fluctuations. This is likely due to the AM signal inter­
ference directly affecting signal power through amplitude variations,
leading to enhanced vertical errors. The FM signal interference causes
even more severe impacts, with the mean error rising to 6.2241 m and
the standard deviation of 5.2630 m. The FM signal interference in­
troduces frequency shifts exacerbating Doppler effects and significantly
increasing error fluctuations in the eastward and northward directions.
The pulse signal interference characterized by periodic bursts of highintensity disruption, results in a mean error of 4.7925 m and a standard
deviation of 3.6242 m. Despite a lower mean error compared to the FM
signal interference, the pulse signal interference significantly affects all
three spatial dimensions. This is due to its transient and repetitive
nature which destabilizes signal reception and disrupts GNSS compu­
tations. These findings demonstrate that different types of signal in­
terferences uniquely affect GNSS-based positioning accuracy. The FM
signal interference exerts the most severe impact followed by pulse and
AM signal interference. The elevation direction is the most sensitive to
such disruptions, highlighting the direct influence of signal interference
characteristics on positioning stability in different directions.

impact of tunnel environments and their transitions on GNSS-based
positioning performance.


**5. Conclusion**


This paper presents a CPNs-based approach to evaluate the perfor­
mances of GNSS-based train positioning systems under real-world op­
erational conditions. By constructing a hierarchical CPN model, the
study successfully models the process of GNSS signal reception and
interferences, as well as the impacts of complex environmental factors
on the GNSS-based train positioning. The simulation results emphasize
that signal interferences (i.e., interfered by AM, FM, and pulse signals)
have a significant impact on the GNSS-based positioning accuracy, with
FM interference exerting the most pronounced effects, particularly in
the vertical direction. Environmental factors like terrain obstructions
and tunnel shielding further degrade signal visibility and increase po­
sitioning errors. The model also identifies critical challenges during
signal reacquisition, particularly in tunnel transitions, which exacerbate
error growth.
While the proposed approach effectively demonstrates the impact of
various signal interferences and environmental conditions on the per­
formance of the GNSS-based train positioning, future research could
explore the integration of additional signal interference mitigation
techniques to improve the accuracy and stability of the GNSS-based
positioning for railway applications.
