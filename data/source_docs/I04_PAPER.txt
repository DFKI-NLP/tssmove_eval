# **Sensors and Microsystems of Wayside Monitoring System for Rolling** **Stock in Motion**

Nencho Nenov [1)], Emil Dimitrov [2)], Nikolay Dodev [1)] and Vasil Vasilev [1) ]

1) Department of Transport Equipment, University of Transport, Sofia, Bulgaria
2) Department of Electronic Technology, Technical University of Sofia, Bulgaria

_**Abstract**_ _:_ _The paper presented is dedicated to the development and application of sensors and_
_microsystems for evaluating various parameters of rolling stock in motion. The intended purpose of_
_such a system, comprising of the described sensors and microsystems and also known as Wayside_
_Monitoring System for Rolling Stock in Motion, is to identify the current overall condition of the_
_railway vehicles, including hot boxes (axle bearings) and brakes (wheel brakes and disc brakes),_
_wheel defects (flat spots, out of roundness, polygonization, etc), dynamic weighing and load_
_distribution, etc. All of these could compromise the safety of the railroad traffic, leading to accidents_
_such as vehicle derailment and increased wear and damages of the tracks._



**1.** **INTRODUCTION**


The working condition under which the railway
vehicles operate could be very severe. This in most
cases includes innumerable elements and activities
that have a detrimental effect on its overall condition
and potentially on the safety of passengers and freight.
The capacity and speeds of the rolling stock also
increases. In order to be cost effective, the railway
traffic should be running smooth and free of accidents
and unscheduled track maintenance.

In order to evaluate the overall condition of the
rolling stock without interrupting the railway work,
the research team is currently developing sensors and
microsystems of wayside monitoring system for the
needs of the Bulgarian National Railway
Infrastructure Company (NRIC). The monitoring
system will be also used by the railway operators and
haulers in order to track their vehicles and loads.
Another intended purpose of the system is the
economic effect by increasing the collection of freight
charges of overloaded vehicles.


**2.** **BASIC PRINCIPLES AND APPROACHES FOR**
**MODELLING OF THE SYSTEM**


The modelling process and building of a wayside
monitoring system includes many factors that need to



be taken into account, such as: basic functions of the
monitoring system, choosing the location of the
check-points, selecting the right method for vehicle
identification, selecting appropriate measuring
methods and sensors, defining procedures and
algorithms about each local check-point, processing
statistical information about alarms, etc.

In order the information obtained through the
monitoring system to be useful for real-time decision
making and the vehicles and loads to be tracked, the
local check-points should be transferring data to
control centers. Taking into account the local
conditions and the remoteness of a check-point, there
are basically two methods for transferring data – by
the fiber-optic network of NRIC (wired) or by the
GSM-R technology (wirelessly). The locations of the
check-points throughout the railway in Bulgaria are
shown on Fig. 1.

For reasons of economy and efficiency the wayside
monitoring system in Bulgaria will feature the
following functions: 1) Identification of a vehicle by
its individual number; 2) Measuring the load of each
wheel – detection of overload, unbalanced vehicles
and uneven load distribution between the wheels and
axles; 3) Detection of wheel defects – flat spots, out of
roundness, polygonizaition, etc.; 4) Detection of hot
boxes, wheels and brakes; 5) Telecommunication
between the local check-points and control centers.


**3.** **SENSORS AND MICROSYSTEMS**


Each of the check-points (Fig. 1) has been
individually equipped with sensors and microsystems,
depending on its designed features. Basically every
check-point needs to be featuring vehicle
identification and telecommunication. But these two
features alone could not deliver useful information
about some of the parameters of the passing trains.
This demands at least one more feature to be added,
e.g. detection of wheel load, detection of wheel
defects or detection of hot boxes, wheels and brakes.

_**3.1. Vehicle identification microsystem**_

The vehicle identification microsystem is crucial
for the wayside monitoring system as all of the
measured parameters of the passing train are linked to
each vehicle of it. For that reason, such a microsystem
should have the ability to work reliably at any
environment conditions. The microsystem should also



be able to accurately read the vehicle’s identification
numbers, plates, signs, etc.

During the decision making about what type of
identification method should the microsystems is best
to use, the research team had made several trials of
different available systems for optical character
recognition (OCR). The results were unsatisfactory
for several reason. The major effect on the OCR was
that the identification numbers, plates, signs, etc. of
the vehicles are not kept in good condition. Most of
the freight vehicles were covered with dirt, rust, etc.
The speed of the passing train was also a problem for
accurate OCR. Adding the weather conditions, such as
raining, snowing, sun light, etc., that made the OCR
method for vehicles identification inapplicable.

Based on the results from the OCR trials, the
research team has decided to use radio frequency
devices to transmit data between each other as the
train passes the check-point – a Rail-TAG and a RailREADER. The Rail-TAG represents an electronic


device, mounted on the vehicle, which stores the
vehicles identification data – Fig. 2 [1]. The
specifications of the Rail-TAG transponder are listed
in Table 1 [1].

_**3.2. Microsystem for wheel load measuring and**_
_**identification of wheel defects**_

The weight of each passing vehicle, the total
weight of the train and the load distribution between
the wheels of a vehicle are measured by means of
specialized strain-gauge force sensors, developed by a
research team of TU Sofia and University of
Transport [2] (Fig. 6). Basically the force sensor
represents a 1140mm long P49E1 rail section with
milled surfaces (sensitive areas) on which a total of
four strain-gauge rosettes are installed. The
connection of the rosettes is shown on Fig. 5.


**Fig. 5.** Full bridge connection of strain-gauge rosettes.

As the output signal of the tensometric bridges is
of very small amplitude, a differential instrumental
amplifier was included [2].

The sensor features two power modes – Low
Power Mode (standby) and Normal Mode
(measuring). The switching between the two modes is
achieved by using rail contacts, installed on the track
before and after the check-point measuring zone. The
rail contacts also serve as axle counters.


**Fig. 6.** Electronics of specialized strain-gauge force
sensor.



The electronics of the specialized force sensor is
contained in a metal box, mounted on the rail section
(Fig. 6).

Specifications of the specialized force sensor are
listed in Table 3.

In order to detect wheel defects, such as flat spots,
out of roundness, polygonization, etc., accelerometer
assemblies are used. The accelerometer assemblies
(Fig. 7) are fixed to the side of the rail and detect peak
amplitudes of the vertical acceleration, induced in the
rail by the passing vehicles. The used accelerometers
are typical 3-axis accelerometers and the vertical
acceleration is measured in the Z-axis. The assemblies
feature two power modes – Low Power Mode
(standby) and Normal Mode (measuring). In Low
Power Mode the power consumption is under 1mA,
and in Normal Mode – 10mA. The switching between
the two modes is also done by the rail contacts, which
controls the power modes of the specialized force
sensor.


**Fig. 7.** Accelerometer assembly for detection of wheel
defects.


_**3.3. Detection of hot boxes, wheels and brakes**_

The detection of hot boxes, wheels and brakes is
achieved by using infrared technology [3]. The
infrared sensors are commercial sensors manufactured
by “voestalpine SIGNALING Siershahn” GmbH. In
order to measure the temperature of the boxes, wheels
and brakes, the microsystem relies on four infrared
sensors, mounted on specialized sleeper on the track.
Two for box temperature measuring (HBD) (Fig. 8)

[3] and two for wheels and brakes temperature
measuring (HWD).

**4.** **INFRASTRUCTURE OF THE WAYSIDE**
**MONITORING SYSTEM**


As Fig. 9 shows, a typical wayside monitoring
system comprises of on-track equipment and road side
equipment. The on-track equipment, depending on the
client request, may include several measurement
microsystems. All of them provide data to the road
side equipment, which comprises a computer system
for data acquisition and data processing. The
measurement results are then transferred to the
information user through local networks or Internet by
using the XML Protocol. The information provided by
the wayside monitoring system is used by the railway
operators, haulers, railway signaling system, etc. in
order to track and evaluate the condition of the
passing vehicles.

As mentioned above, the initiation of the
measuring process of all the microsystems is triggered
by rail contacts. They represent simple strain-gauge
force sensors, installed on the track before and after
the check-point zone. Their signal is used by the roadside equipment for triggering the process for the ontrack equipment. The axle counting is crucial for
identification and linking the measured data to the
corresponding vehicle and its axles.

After the last axle in the train has passed the rail
contact, installed after the check-point zone, the data
measuring process is stopped and the acquired data is
processed by the road-side equipment. Depending on
the train length, the processing time may vary, but no
more than couple of minutes.

After the processing has been completed, the roadside equipment sends the results, along with alarms,
remarks, etc. to the information users by the local
network (fiber-optics) or Internet (wirelessly through
GSM-R technology). In order to minimize the amount
of data to be transferred, the system uses the XML
Protocol. Depending on the type of information
needed by the corresponding user, the data from
transferred XML file is used to build up service
databases, protocols, summaries, etc. The alarms
about identified critical irregularities in a passing train
are sent with priority to the next train station and the
signaling systems in order the train to be stopped and
checked before continuing.

In case of power interruption, the system is backed
up by alternative power supply (UPS) for couple of
hours before complete shutdown.
