**Abstract**


Predictive maintenance (PdM) is a critical tool in aviation, promoting sustainability, safety, and cost-effectiveness. However, a
significant challenge in implementing predictive maintenance framework is limited run-to-failure data due to frequent preventive
maintenance. Generative AI (GAI) offers a promising solution to this challenge by generating synthetic data, enabling more
accurate predictions of aircraft system health. The recent surge in scientific publications exploring GAI's potential for aviation
predictive maintenance emphasizes the need for a comprehensive review. This research addresses this gap by conducting a
systematic literature review (SLR), employing an active learning open-source tool to thoroughly analyze papers sourced from
four scientific databases. These papers focus on predictive maintenance of aircraft systems through generative AI models. The
findings of this review examine various aspects of GAI-powered predictive maintenance, including its objectives, the diversity
of models employed, areas of application, datasets used for model validation, and the prevailing challenges and emerging trends.
The review identified that most commonly used GAI models in aircraft PdM are GANs (Generative Adversarial Networks),
VAEs (Variational Autoencoders) or combination of these models. However, knowledge sharing, model integration and
specificity are the key challenges associated with the implementation of these models. This strong knowledge-base will be a
valuable resource for researchers, engineers, and practitioners aiming to improve their knowledge and implementation of GAIdriven models for predictive maintenance in the aviation industry.

**Keywords** : Generative AI, Predictive Maintenance (PdM), Aircraft Maintenance, Generative Adversarial Network, Data

**1.** **Introduction**


Maintenance plays a pivotal role in the aviation industry,
with its importance deeply rooted in ensuring the reliability
and safety of aircraft operations. Operating in an
environment characterized by extreme temperatures, high
altitudes, and various forces during flight, aircraft systems
undergo age-related degradation and operational wear
without regular maintenance. The repercussions of such
neglect are severe, leading to system failures that can
compromise the safety of passengers and crew on board [1].
Conversely, the financial ramifications of grounded aircraft
due to insufficient maintenance are substantial, compelling
airlines to prioritize comprehensive maintenance programs

[2].



Regular maintenance actions not only strengthen the
structural and operational integrity of aircraft but also extend
the lifespan of critical equipment, mitigating revenue losses
associated with unplanned downtime [3]. According to a
report by Statista, the financial burden of unscheduled
aircraft maintenance is anticipated to skyrocket in the
aviation industry, reaching an estimated annual cost of nearly
39 billion USD by 2035 [4]. This projection is particularly
alarming considering the expected surge in the global fleet
size, forecasted to almost double from 25,900 to 47,080
aircraft between 2019 and 2041, due to increased demand for
both passenger and cargo transportation [5]. As aging planes
with limited sensor capabilities are retired, a significant
surge in maintenance requirements is inevitable, potentially
overwhelming existing data management systems under the


deluge of data from modern aircraft. This confluence of
trends demands innovative solutions to ensure safe and
efficient air travel in the coming decades.
In the aviation industry, various approaches to
maintenance are practiced and adopted. The literature offers
a variety of maintenance management approaches with
diverse terminology. However, Adryan & Sastra [6] put
together most common classification of these practices:
Reactive approach is the most basic approach, which
involves fixing problems only after they arise. Preventive
approach on the other hand entails scheduling maintenance
tasks based on equipment operating hours or predetermined
time intervals. Predictive approach, which is the focus of this
study, utilizes data analysis and monitoring techniques to
predict potential equipment failures before they happen.
These maintenance strategies determine the types of
maintenance activities and their timing to be undertaken in
the decision-making process. Nonetheless, because of the
growing volume of data being gathered by the onboard
sensors of contemporary aircraft, the aviation sector is
aggressively utilizing predictive maintenance, or PdM [7].
The analysis of data from a machine's performance,
condition, and other health indicators is the core goal of
predictive maintenance. By optimizing the time between
repairs, this technique assists in determining the best time for
maintenance. Unlike traditional maintenance schedules,
PdM uses sensor networks to monitor the machine and
trigger maintenance actions only when pre-defined
conditions indicate a potential failure [8]. An International
Air Transport Association (IATA) report from 2015 suggests
that predictive maintenance could potentially increase
aircraft availability by up to 35% [9]. This increased
availability directly translates to reduced overall
maintenance costs.
Researchers have identified three main approaches to
predictive maintenance: physical modeling, data-driven, and
hybrid [10]. Physics-based modelling uses existing
knowledge of a system to create mathematical models that
predict how it will degrade over time [11]. However,
building such models can be time-consuming and
computationally expensive, especially for complex systems
with numerous interacting components [10]. In contrast,
data-driven approach focuses on monitoring a system's
condition and learning from historical data to predict its
future state. Utilizing reliability functions, statistics, and
even artificial intelligence, this approach finds possible
equipment problems before they turn into serious
malfunctions. However, the quantity and quality of the data
gathered are crucial to the prediction's accuracy because
inaccurate or insufficient data might provide erroneous
models [12]. Finally, the hybrid approach combines these
methods. The recent surge in machine data, combined with
the power of deep learning, is significantly improving the
accuracy of PdM models. This leads to more targeted
maintenance strategies, focusing resources on the



components most likely to fail, ultimately leading to
optimized maintenance plans [8].
As industrial demands for efficiency and reliability are
pushing traditional maintenance methods to their limits,
Prognostics and Health Management (PHM) has emerged as
a powerful solution in aviation industry [10], in which PdM
is also a key element [12]. By analyzing real-time and
historical data, PHM can assess aircraft equipment health and
predict future issues. Machine learning, a key tool within
PHM, utilizes algorithms trained on vast amounts of data to
predict equipment health or remaining useful life (RUL) [6].
However, a major hurdle lies in the need for labeled data
(data with known outcomes) for training these algorithms

[13]. Limited labeled data can significantly restrict the
effectiveness of machine learning in PdM [14].
Acquiring the ideal data for machine learning in PdM for
aircraft is practically challenging. This data, commonly
known as "run-to-failure" data, consists of time-series
readings that capture the gradual degradation of a component

- from a healthy state all the way to complete failure [15].
The problem lies in the extended timelines involved. For
instance, a seemingly minor issue in a critical aircraft part
might take months or even years to progress to complete
failure under normal operating conditions. However,
allowing a component to reach this point is not only
unacceptable but also poses a serious safety risk. To
prioritize safety, aircraft parts are routinely replaced well
before reaching a critical failure state. Researchers are
actively addressing this challenge by developing generative
AI models that can predict future equipment health even with
scarce failure data [16].
In order to improve PdM procedures in the aviation
sector and get beyond data constraints, generative AI (GAI)
models are an effective technique. These algorithms may
create synthetic data that imitates real-world "run-to-failure"
circumstances after being trained on available data, even if it
is incomplete. This synthetic data can then be used to train
other machine learning algorithms for PdM tasks. Therefore,
machine learning models may be able to forecast equipment
health and RUL more accurately with more extensive
training data. A variety of methods are included in generative
AI, such as generative adversarial networks (GANs) and
variational autoencoders (VAEs). VAEs generate unique and
credible data points by examining underlying patterns in the
data [8]. GANs, on the other hand, employ a collaborative
competition between two neural networks. One network
generates data, while the other strives to differentiate
between real and generated data. These adversarial dynamic
drives the continual improvement of both networks,
ultimately leading to the generation of increasingly realistic
synthetic data [13].

**1.1** **Research Gap and Related Work**


Over the past decade, several reviews have explored
predictive maintenance applications across industries


through various approaches. For instance, Ran et al. [14] and
Zhang et al. [12] examined fault diagnosis, prognosis, and
machine/deep learning techniques in PdM for diverse
industrial settings. While these reviews offered valuable
insights, they lacked a specific focus on the aircraft industry.
Similarly, Carvalho et al. [15] provided an extensive review
of various machine learning methods and datasets for PdM
applications, highlighting approaches for specific
equipment. However, their review, like the others, did not
explore applications specific to the aviation industry.
Although, some reviews addressed aircraft applications,
but lacked specifics regarding generative AI models for
predictive maintenance. Fu et al. [10] reviewed data-driven
prognostic algorithms, including conventional and deep
learning models. They highlighted aircraft as a key PdM
application and reviewed papers using different models for
various aircraft equipment. However, their focus was not on
generative AI. Similarly, Serradilla et al. [8] reviewed deep
learning architectures for PdM stages like anomaly detection
and RUL estimation, using aircraft systems as examples.
Although they discussed generative models in general but
lacked details pertaining to their applications in aircraft PdM.
Adryan & Sastra [6] reviewed machine learning for aircraft
engine PdM, concluding that LSTM and GRU models deliver
high accuracy (over 99%). Abdulrahman et al. [17] explored
deep learning models for detecting aero-engine blade
defects, identifying CNN and YOLO as popular choices.
They also highlighted challenges like data scarcity and the
inability to detect new defects, but generative AI was not the
focus. Finally, Wen et al. [18] reviewed data-driven
prognostic algorithms, similar to Fu et al. [10], with a focus
on aircraft applications. However, generative AI models
were not addressed.
The closest study to generative AI applications in PdM
comes from Sabuhi et al. [13]. They explored various
domains like medicine and security, demonstrating
generative AI's potential. However, their work lacked an
aviation industry focus, highlighting the gap in research on
generative AI for predictive maintenance in the aviation
sector.
In essence, existing systematic reviews offer valuable
insights into PdM using various techniques, but they lack a
dedicated focus on generative AI models within the specific
context of aviation predictive maintenance. This gap presents
a compelling opportunity for further research to explore the
potential and unique functionalities of generative AI in
enhancing predictive maintenance practices for the aviation
industry.
Keeping in view the above stated facts, to the best of
author’s knowledge, this study stands out from previous
research in two ways. First, it strictly followed a wellestablished method called SLR protocol. Second, it narrowed
the focus to the specific combination of generative AI and
predictive maintenance within the aviation industry. By
focusing on a specific niche and employing a systematic
approach, this study delivers unique and valuable insights.

**2.4** **Search Method**


This thesis adopted the systematic literature review
methodology outlined by Preferred Reporting Items for
Systematic Reviews and Meta-Analysis (PRISMA),
thoroughly following a step-by-step process. The PRISMA
framework is widely adopted by researchers for its ability to
provide a scientifically rigorous structure for reporting
methodology, results, and the conclusions drawn from their
work [21]. This process involves comprehensive
identification, evaluation, and review of relevant
publications within the chosen field of study. Moreover,
PRISMA employs a flow diagram consisting of four phases,



which visually depicts the process of selecting research
papers during the systematic review [22].
Additionally, to ensure an efficient and comprehensive
search, automated searches within each chosen electronic
database were conducted. This involved utilizing a carefully
formulated search string, as discussed above. The search
string served as a precise filter, scanning vast databases for
studies that contained the key terms and concepts most
relevant to the research questions. By automating this
process for each database, a significant volume of potentially
relevant studies was identified in a timely manner.

  - **Semi-Automated Screening.** One of the biggest
bottlenecks in systematic reviews is the process of selecting
relevant studies after basic filtering based on abstracts. This
task is known to be laborious and monotonous, vulnerable to
mistakes, and prohibitively time intensive [21]. To address
this challenge, researchers are increasingly turning to datadriven tools to support in the selection phase [21]. This study
utilized ASReview Lab (version 1.5), an Active Learning
tool developed by Utrecht University [23]. Active learning is
a machine learning technique where the algorithm
specifically selects data points for a human reviewer to
analyze, focusing on those that are most likely to be
informative. In the context of systematic reviews, this
translates to the tool prioritizing studies that are most likely
to be relevant to the research question [24].
There were several reasons why ASReview Lab was
chosen for this study. First, it is open source, making it freely
available to researchers. Second, ASReview runs locally,
ensuring that the sensitive data being analyzed remains
private. Third, the interface is user-friendly and easy to
learn, even for researchers without prior experience with the
tool [23].
To use ASReview Lab, it was first installed using the
instructions provided on the website. Next, all articles
retrieved for the review, previously managed in Zotero, were
exported in RIS file format and imported into ASReview. As
the dataset required screening from the scratch, Oracle mode
with unlabeled data was chosen. This mode assumes no prior
classifications exist within the dataset. Active learning cycles
rely on initial training data, also known as prior knowledge.
This information serves as the foundation for the classifier to
establish an initial ranking of the unreviewed studies. In this
instance, two relevant and three irrelevant records were
provided to the tool to jumpstart this process. Moreover, the
author opted to utilize the default machine learning model,
Naive Bayes, for the initial prioritization of studies.
ASReview then presented the author with a series of articles
ranked by their likelihood of being relevant. After each
article was reviewed and a decision made (included or



excluded), the machine learning model was updated,
allowing it to refine its ranking of the remaining studies. The
review process continued until encountering 22 irrelevant
articles in a row, prompting the author to stop at 122 articles
reviewed. This represents 61.30% reading of the initial pool
of retrieved studies. Finally, through this semi-automated
abstract screening process, the number of relevant studies
was reduced from 199 to 57 (Column 3, Table 3).

**2.6** **Data Extraction**


Systematic reviews rely on accurate data extraction from
primary studies. To minimize bias and ensure consistency,
data extraction forms should be developed and tested
alongside the overall study protocol. This ensures the forms
effectively capture all the necessary information from the
outset, reducing the potential for subjective interpretation
during data extraction [19]. This study adopted a data
extraction approach learned by the guidelines of Kitchenham
and Charters [19] and the process used by Tummers et al.

[25]. The process began with reading three randomly
selected articles. Based on these papers, key data points that
were needed to be captured were defined. The form was then
tested by applying it to five more randomly selected studies
and extracting their data. This iterative process resulted in
the final data extraction form as specified in Table 6. After
extracting data from all 16 studies using the finalized form,
it was analyzed using Microsoft Excel to identify potential
trends.

imputation techniques involve filling in the blanks in the
data. Unlike traditional estimation techniques of statistical
analysis, neural network methods work in two stages. First,
they train a model on the data. Then, in the imputation stage,
the trained model is used to "fill in the blanks" for missing
values by generating data that follows those learned patterns

[29].
Aircraft generate a lot of data through sensors installed
but the key problem in implementing PdM is that the data is
imbalanced. This means that normal operation readings
significantly outnumber anomaly data. This imbalance
mirrors situations where the number of healthy samples
vastly outweighs defective ones, or the training data fails to
represent all fault categories equally. Consequently, GAI
models can become biased towards the over-represented
normal class, leading to misleading performance metrics like
accuracy. To address this challenge, various techniques are
being used to balance the data before training the models.
This involved oversampling the rare "failure" data or under
sampling the abundant "normal" data. Wu et al. [30], Chen et
al. [31], Chen et al. [28], Liu et al. [32], Xiong et al. [33] and
Fu et al. [34] addressed class imbalance in datasets using
generative models. They improved classification accuracy in
fault diagnosis and yielded a more balanced dataset by
creating synthetic fault data for training diagnostic models.
Similarly, Zhang et al. [35], Lv et al. [36], Lang et al. [37],
Xiong et al. [33] and Zhang et al. [28] handled the sparse
degradation data by creating synthetic time-series data
representing different degradation patterns. This data
augmentation enriched training datasets for machine learning
models, allowing them to identify these early warning signs
and predict RUL more accurately. In some cases, sensor data
might also be incomplete, with missing readings. Ma et al.

[29] proposed that generative models can reconstruct missing
entries for time-series data with missing values from multiple
sensors, leading to improved downstream tasks like RUL
prediction in aeroengines. Furthermore, Huang et al. [38]
used variational auto encoders to improve the feature
extraction process. They stated that AI models can go beyond
simple data augmentation by uncovering hidden patterns
within sensor data and generated health index curve, leading
to better RUL prediction accuracy.

**3.4.1** **Generative Adversarial Network.** A typical GAN
consist of two neural networks; a generator that creates new
data resembling real data and a discriminator that tries to
distinguish between real and generated data. The generator
tries to improve its ability to create realistic data, while the
discriminator tries to perform better at differentiating
between the real and the fake data, through an iterative
training process. This adversarial training helps the generator
produce increasingly realistic data.
In this study, eleven variants of GANs were identified to
address fault diagnosis of aircraft parts. A semi-supervised
Information Maximizing Generative Adversarial Network
(SS-InfoGAN) has been employed by Wu et al. [30] on
classification of bearing faults. SS-InfoGAN uses labeled
data to not only generate realistic time series but also retain
class information. This allows the model to generate samples
specifically for minority classes. Interpretable Temporal
GANs (IT-GANs) were proposed by Huang et al. [39],
especially for imbalanced multivariate time series flight data.
Through the process of learning variable association graphs
for every class, IT-GANs integrate an interpretability feature.
This allows for understanding the differentiating factors
between different classes. Chen et al. [31] utilizes a Deep
Residual Network-Auxiliary Classifier GAN (DRN

ACGAN) for fault diagnosis of rolling bearings. The DRN
architecture within the generator helps address the vanishing
gradient problem associated with deep networks, leading to
an improved quality of the generated samples. Additionally,
Instance Normalization (IN) is incorporated to stabilize the
training process.
Lv et al. [36] employed SAAN model for RUL
prediction of aeroengines using sensor data. The generator
adapts the auxiliary data to resemble the degradation patterns
of the target engine. It fools the discriminator by
transforming the auxiliary data to appear like target engine
data while preserving the RUL information. While the
discriminator tries to correctly identify the data source and
predict the sequence life and RUL for both the target engine
data and the generator's outputs. The LSTM layer in the
discriminator is shared with the RUL prediction task. This
allows the model to leverage features learned for source
identification and sequence life prediction to improve RUL
prediction accuracy.
Duan et al. [40] used the improved Conditional GANs
(CGAN) to address sparse data issues for RUL prediction.
Unlike regular GANs, CGAN takes an additional input called
a conditional variable and specifically handle time series data
like condition monitoring samples. The model is applied to
Aircraft Auxiliary Power Unit (APU) as an example of a
complex electro-mechanical system. The paper further
employed various RUL prediction methods like Support
Vector Regression (SVR) and Restricted Boltzmann
Machine - Extreme Learning Machine (RBM-ELM) along
with the augmented data for better prediction.
Baptista et al. [41] proposed a 1D-Denoising GAN (1DDGAN-PHM) to remove noise from 1-dimensional condition
monitoring signals, while preserving important edges
(degradation patterns) in the data. This model is applied to
data from a simulated aircraft turbofan engine (NASA's CMAPSS simulator). The model consists of a denoising
generator and a discriminator. The generator learns to
remove noise from the input signal. The discriminator helps
the generator learn by comparing noise-free signals with the
generator's outputs. The specific architecture uses stride
convolutions instead of pooling layers, batch normalization,
LeakyReLU (activation function used to introduce nonlinearity among the outputs) activation, and a final fully
connected layer in the discriminator. The generator uses an
autoencoder structure to enhance its denoising capability. It
is trained to minimize both the reconstruction error
(difference between noisy input and generated clean signal)
and the adversarial loss (fooling the discriminator into
thinking the generated signal is real).
Chen et al. [28] proposed an enhanced fault diagnosis
method using a Pre-Trained Wasserstein GAN with Gradient
Penalty (PT-WGAN-GP). WGAN is a variant of GAN that
seeks to eliminate issues like mode collapse, increase
learning stability, and produce meaningful learning curves
that are helpful for debugging and hyperparameter searches.
The proposed model aims to generate high-quality samples



and expand the imbalanced training dataset for improved
fault diagnosis. The model consists of a pre-training network,
discriminator, classifier and a generator. The pre-trained
network is trained on different data sources (balanced data)
to improve the generalization ability of the diagnostic model.
This network is then used as the initial layers for both the
discriminator and classifier. The discriminator uses the pretrained network and helps the generator learn by
distinguishing real fault signals from generated ones. The
classifier uses the pre-trained network and classifies the fault
types based on the processed signals (either real or
generated). The generator incorporates Instance
Normalization (IN) and Residual Network. This network is
designed to generate new, realistic fault signals that resemble
the imbalanced data. Instance normalization is used instead
of Batch Normalization (BN) to provide better ability of
fitting with imbalanced data.
Xiong et al. [33] tackles the difficulty of predicting RUL
when real-world operating conditions are missing from the
training data. To address this, they propose a novel
framework called Controlled Physics Informed GAN (CPIGAN). CPI-GAN is a physics-informed generative
adversarial network that offers two key advantages. First, it
controls the generation of TTF trajectories (time-to-failure)
by combining GANs with RNNs to capture long-term
dependencies and incorporating physical laws for realistic
and interpretable degradation patterns. Second, it ensures the
generated data aligns with the actual degradation process
through a physics-informed loss function based on inferred
system health indicators. This combination of controlled
generation and physics-informed loss function allows CPIGAN to produce synthetic data that reflects real-world
conditions, improving the accuracy of RUL prediction.
Lang et al. [37] and Zhang et al. [35] combines the
strength of three distinct neural networks namely
Convolutional Neural Network (CNN) and Convolutional
Recurrent Neural Network (CNN) like Long Short-term
Memory Network (LSTM) and GAN to address the data
limitation of training data for predicting aircraft engine RUL.
The GAN augments the training data, whereas CNN-LSTM
focus on extracting key features relevant to predicting faults
and capture long-term dependencies in sequence data.
Similarly, Huang et al. [38] uses GAN-LSTM to improve the
RUL prediction accuracy of aero-engines.
Ma et al. [29] introduces TSGAIN (Time-Series
Generative Adversarial Imputation Network) with PPCTE
(Pre-imputation, Parallel Convolution, Transformer
Encoder) to address the challenge of missing values in multisensor aircraft engine data. TSGAIN builds upon GAN to
impute missing data, but it incorporates several
advancements. A pre-imputation module refines the initial
data fed into the system. To capture the complex
relationships between multiple sensors, a parallel
convolution module is introduced. Finally, a transformer
encoder with a multi-head attention mechanism strengthens


the model's ability to learn temporal dependencies within the
sensor readings.

**3.4.2** **Auto-Encoders.** Another prominent type of neural
network architecture identified in SLR is Auto-Encoders.
These models aim at learning compressed representations of
raw data through a unique training process. The encoder acts
like a compression engine, transforming the input data into a
lower-dimensional latent space representation. This space
captures the main features of the original data in a more
compact form. The decoder then uses this compressed data
to reconstruct the original input data as accurately as
possible. The training process focuses on minimizing the
reconstruction error, which is the difference between the
original input data and the reconstructed output generated by
the decoder. By iteratively refining the encoder and decoder
based on this error, the AE learns to extract meaningful and
informative features from the data [42].
Peng et al. [42] employed a novel method of Variational
Auto-Encoders Generative Adversarial Networks (VAEGANs) for predicting RUL for turbofan engines using sensor
data. The model consists of an encoder that reduces the
dimensionality of sensor data, extracting hidden features, a
decoder / generator that generates HI degradation curves that
resemble real curves based on the encoded data and a
discriminator that improves the generated HI curves by
distinguishing them from real ones, promoting realistic and
characteristic degradation patterns. Then it uses a Bidirectional Long Short-Term Memory network (BLSTM) for
prediction of sensor sequence. It incorporated two LSTMs in
opposite directions to Capture both past and future
dependencies in the sensor data sequences. The model finally
combines the predicted sensor data with predicted HI curve
and extrapolates the Health Index curve to a predefined
threshold value. The RUL is predicted through the difference
between the cycle reaching the threshold and the current
cycle.
Zhang et al. [27] proposes the framework named DataAugmentation-boosted Dual Informer framework
(DARWIN) for predicting performance degradation in aeroengines. DARWIN addresses the challenge of limited data in
the initial degradation stages, a critical period for early fault
detection. The framework employs a two-step approach.
First, a data augmentation model based on the Informer
architecture generates additional degradation time-series
data. Second, a separate Informer model with a generative
decoder is employed for prediction. Additionally, a specific
padding strategy is implemented to preserve the integrity of
the degradation context during the prediction process.

**3.4.3** **Other Statistical Techniques integrated with**
**Neural Networks.** Few hybrid methods were identified
during the study which employed data processing techniques
coupled with deep learning models to achieve fault
diagnosis. Liu et al. [32] focuses on anomaly detection of gas
turbines and addressed the class imbalance (fewer anomaly



samples) and overlapping features between normal and
anomaly data. They introduced deep attention synthetic
minority over-sampling technique with the Encoder-Decoder
(DA-SMOTE-ED), which utilizes a hybrid re-sampling
scheme, strategically removing some normal data (undersampling) while preserving informative examples. The core
lies in advanced data augmentation with feature learning. A
self-attention-based Encoder-Decoder tackles both interclass overlap by learning a well-separated feature space and
temporal feature extraction from multi-dimensional sensor
readings. To ensure high-quality synthetic anomaly data,
DA-SMOTE-ED incorporates an attention module that
dynamically controls the "blending" between existing
anomalies.
The study by Fu et al. [34] proposed a novel approach
for diagnosing faults in aviation hydraulic pumps. They
addressed the scarcity of fault samples and integrates local
wavelet similarity fusion (LWSF) with a CNN in a method
called LWSF_CNN. It works by decomposing vibration
signals into wavelet packet coefficients (WPCs) which
contain characteristic information at specific frequencies.
LWSF then modifies the WPCs of faulty samples by
referencing normal samples, ensuring the generated samples
remain representative of the fault. Finally, the modified
WPCs are used to reconstruct new, augmented faulty
samples, increasing the diversity and number of samples for
improved fault classification. The CNN is then trained on
this balanced dataset and is used to diagnose faults in unseen
test samples.

**3.5** **RQ-3: Which application domains exist**
**in GAI based Aircraft PdM?**


Recent years have seen a surge in innovative methods
for PdM in aviation sector, with many researchers employing
a combination of data-driven analysis and machine learning
techniques. Below is an overview of the various PdM
applications within the aircraft industry. Aero-engine and
rolling bearings have been identified to be the leading
applications in aircraft where GAI has reached a more mature
stage.

**3.5.1** **Aero-Engine.** Aircraft engines are complex
machinery and a significant expense for airlines, requiring
regular maintenance to ensure smooth operation. These
engines rely on a network of sensors to monitor their health,
including factors like fan inlet temperature, pressure, and
rotational speed. Aircraft engine failures can be catastrophic,
so ensuring their reliability is crucial [42]. Predicting RUL
of an aircraft engine involves analyzing complex multivariate time series data of sensors collected throughout the
engine's lifecycle, which makes it as one of the most
challenging tasks in PHM [37].
Zhang et al. [27] describe the three stages of an engine’s
life in their paper: healthy, initial degradation, and severe
degradation. The healthy stage has no degradation trends and


random sensor readings. The initial degradation stage,
however, holds the key to identifying impending failure, but
data from this stage is scarce [27, 36]. Existing methods that
do not differentiate these stages can lead to false alarms or
even accidents. Majority of papers have talked about the
challenges which are faced in acquiring degradation data of
aircraft. Obtaining enough information on complete engine
failures is a significant hurdle. It could take years for an
engine such as turbofan engine to naturally degrade from its
peak performance to a complete breakdown. In practical
scenarios, experiencing failure is highly unfavorable as it
poses safety risks and carries the potential for severe and
irreversible damage. As a consequence, engine parts are
replaced long before they reach the point of failure,
prioritizing safety over the collection of complete
degradation data. This lack of "run-to-failure" data, which is
particularly valuable because it captures the engine's natural
degradation process in its entirety creates a major challenge
for advanced machine learning algorithms trying to
accurately predict an engine's remaining useful life

[29,33,35,38].
Existing reviews on aircraft engine health have
highlighted the potential of machine learning, particularly for
predicting RUL. The methods proposed by researchers often
combine existing RUL models with techniques for
generating realistic time-series data. These AI networks
excel at finding patterns in data, helping them understand the
relationships between various sensor measurements.
Additionally, they can learn from sequences, allowing them
to capture how sensor readings change over time [35].

**3.5.2** **Rolling Bearings** . Bearings minimize frictional
forces between parts engaged in relative motion along a
predetermined axis. For high-end equipment, condition
monitoring and diagnosing bearing health is a critical aspect
of PHM. In the aviation industry, high-speed bearings within
an aeroengine's rotor system are undeniably crucial for
ensuring the safe and stable operation of the aircraft.
However, these bearings are susceptible to various faults due
to the harsh environments they endure during extended
operation. These faults can manifest as vibrations caused by
rotor issues like misalignment, rubbing or loose components.
Ultimately, bearing faults can lead to equipment failure,
compromising sustained airworthiness, and potentially
causing significant financial losses or even catastrophic
accidents [28].
Vibration signals, due to their ease of use and wide
frequency range, are a critical indicator of bearing health.
However, diagnosing faults through these signals can be
difficult. The complexity of bearing fault modes, such as
cracks, wear, and lubrication problems, leads to vibration
signals that are erratic, non-linear, and constantly changing.
Aeroengine rotor systems present unique challenges for
intelligent diagnostic methods. Rolling bearings in these
systems operate continuously under harsh conditions – high
speeds, high temperatures, and variable workloads. The



complex combination of centrifugal, aerodynamic, and
thermal loads further complicates diagnosis [28]. A
significant hurdle stems from the imbalanced nature of the
data. Bearings typically operate in a normal state for
extended periods, resulting in far more data for healthy states
compared to faulty ones. This imbalance can negatively
impact the performance of data-driven intelligent diagnostic
methods [31]. Therefore, improving prediction efficiency in
the presence of data imbalance, particularly for minority
classes holds immense importance [30].

**3.5.3** **Hydraulics.** The health of hydraulic pumps
significantly impacts aircraft safety and reliability as
hydraulic systems are critical for aircraft flight controls.
However, harsh operating environment pose serious
challenges to the health of these pumps. Early fault detection
is crucial to prevent malfunctions.
Diagnosing faults in these pumps is challenging.
Building accurate physical models requires extensive
knowledge, and traditional model-based methods are limited
by this knowledge gap. Recent advances in data-driven fault
diagnosis, particularly deep learning, offer promise. These
techniques use historical pump data to train automated fault
classifiers, directly learning features indicative of faults from
real-world data. However, a major hurdle exists similar to
that of bearing as discussed above: data imbalance. Because
aviation hydraulic pumps are highly reliable, collecting data
on faulty pumps is difficult. Pumps spend most of their time
operating normally, resulting in a vast amount of normal data
compared to a very limited set of fault data (sometimes as
few as 2-3 samples). This imbalance significantly hinders the
effectiveness of data-driven methods.
Only one publication related to fault diagnosis of
hydraulic pump was found in this review. The same study by
Fu et al. [34] proposed a solution that capitalized on the
similarities between normal and faulty vibration signals. By
exploiting these shared features, the model was enhanced to
effectively "augment" the limited faulty samples, reducing
reliance on the small original dataset.

**3.5.4** **Auxiliary Power Unit.** The Auxiliary Power Unit
is a critical electro-mechanical component of an aircraft, but
its complex and non-linear degradation process makes it
difficult to predict RUL using traditional data-driven
methods. Most of the condition monitoring samples collected
from the APU during aircraft operation lack degradation
features and are unusable for RUL prediction [40].
Duan et al. [40] state in their work that even after preprocessing, only a small portion of the data is suitable for
analysis. Moreover, they highlighted that for the APU, only
exhaust gas temperature (EGT) and bleed air pressure (PT)
are considered informative for RUL prediction. These twodimensional samples might not be enough for accurate
prediction, so improved Conditional Generative Adversarial
Networks (CGAN) is used to augment the data.


**3.5.5** **Gas Turbine.** A gas turbine is a critical
component of many aircraft engines. Most gas turbine data
reflect normal operation, with only a small portion indicating
anomalies. Additionally, abnormal data can closely resemble
normal data before an issue arises. These factors make
anomaly detection of gas turbines difficult. Moreover, gas
turbine data is complex, multi-dimensional time series with
intricate relationships between various parameters.
Traditional methods struggle to extract meaningful
information from this data to create synthetic abnormal data
for analysis [32].
While numerous gas turbine faults exist, Liu et al. (2023)
highlights that key performance parameters for anomaly
detection include exhaust gas temperature (EGT), rotor
speeds (N1 and N2), and fuel flow (FF). However, these
parameters are influenced by flight conditions and
environment. To account for this, manufacturers may
transform these raw measurements into derived metrics that
are less sensitive to external variations, ultimately improving
the accuracy of anomaly detection systems.

**3.6** **RQ-4: Which datasets are used to**
**validate GAI based Models in aircraft PdM?**


Data-driven prognostics is playing an increasingly
important role in aircraft health monitoring with the growing
availability of sensor data and sophisticated algorithms. This
approach analyzes sensor measurements from various
subsystems to assess their health, pinpointing patterns that
indicate potential issues. Previously, limited access to realworld data hampered PdM research. However, the recent rise
of public datasets for machinery has provided an extensive
collection of sensor measurement data. This allows
researchers to develop and test PdM algorithms using realworld conditions, leading to a surge in experimentation. This
section explores the datasets employed in PdM tasks, as
identified in recent scientific literature.
Publicly available datasets play a pivotal role within the
realm of scientific research. They enable researchers to share
critical data and facilitate the comparative analysis of
methodologies across various disciplines. However, in the
specific domain of aircraft maintenance research, a single
dataset dominates: The National Aeronautics and Space
Administration's (NASA) Commercial Modular AeroPropulsion System Simulation (C-MAPSS). This dataset
offers high-fidelity simulations of large commercial turbofan
jet engines, incorporating realistic engine control systems.
Each data point combines multiple measurements taken over
time forming multivariate time series from 21 sensors.
Additionally, the dataset consists of four sub datasets. These
sub datasets represent different operational conditions and
fault scenarios for the simulated turbofan engines, commonly
identified by codes like FD001, FD002, FD003, and FD004.
The data represents a diverse fleet of engines with varying
degrees of initial wear and potentially different
manufacturing processes. It encompasses information on 100



such engines, including how their performance changes over
time until they fail called run-to-failure time-series
trajectories [36,38].
While the C-MAPSS dataset is a popular choice for
researchers because it allows for easy comparisons between
different models, it has limitations. Because it focuses on
turbofan engines, most research uses it for that specific
component. This means there's less research on other critical
airplane parts, like hydraulics and bearings, which require
different data and analysis techniques. In other words,
researchers were found to be focusing too much on one area
because the data is readily available, neglecting other
important parts of an airplane.
For conducting fault diagnosis methods related to rolling
bearings, the researchers were found to leverage a publicly
available bearing dataset from Paderborn University. This
dataset, collected in 2016, is particularly valuable because it
incorporates real-world complexities. It contains vibration
data from 30 bearings: 6 healthy, 12 with artificially induced
faults, and 14 with real-world damage such as wear and tear
through accelerated life tests. This variety of damage types,
encompassing single point faults, distributed faults, and even
combinations, allows for a more robust evaluation of the
method's effectiveness in handling diverse bearing fault
scenarios [28].
Another bearing dataset from the Polytechnic University
of Turin in Italy was found to be used for testing of PdM
models. This dataset focuses on high-speed bearings
employed in aeroengines and includes vibration acceleration
data measured under varying rotational speeds (18,000 and
24,000 RPM) and across different levels of bearing damage.
This allows the researchers to assess how well their fault
diagnosis method performs in identifying issues within these
high-speed bearings under diverse operating conditions [31].
Other experimental datasets found in the literature were
based on test rigs and practical settings. Fu et al. [34] used
ground-based test rig simulating an aviation hydraulic pump
to collect vibration signals during fault simulations.
Similarly, Wu et al. [30] investigated bearing faults using a
rotating machinery platform. Duan et al. [40] validated their
method for estimating remaining useful life in a practical
setting using real-world sensor readings from the APU on an
Airbus A320 aircraft during its environmental control mode.
Likewise, Huang et al. [39] investigated real-world airplane
data to detect malfunctions. They collected comprehensive
flight data from 47 sensors on each aircraft throughout the
entire flight (takeoff to landing). This data includes
information on both normal flights and those experiencing
two specific types of problems.

**4.** **Discussion**


This study is the first comprehensive SLR to explore
how Generative AI can be used for predicting maintenance
needs in aircraft. We looked at over 400 research papers from
2019 and narrowed it down to 16 high-quality studies. An


active learning process was implemented using ASReview
Lab to select relevant articles based on abstracts. The process
was stopped after the appearance of 22 consecutive irrelevant
articles. The selection process could have been stopped
earlier but that would have slightly increased the risk of
losing relevant articles.
Notably, all papers clearly stated the objectives of
addressing the challenge of limited run-to-failure data of
through generative models. Data scarcity, imbalanced
dataset, noisy and missing data are some key challenges
posed to accurate predictions of RUL and fault diagnosis

[28,29,30,32,35,37,41,42]. The most popular models
employed for GAI based PdM are GANs and VAEs.
Different variants of GANs, such as CGANs, DGANs,
WGANs, RNN-GANs, CR-GANs etc. are being adopted by
researchers to address the issues of limited data. However,
the choice of the specific GAN variant is dependent on the
nature of the data and the desired level of interpretability.
VAEs, on the other hand, are being utilized to extract
meaningful and information features from the data. All the
proposed approaches, combining under-sampling, feature
space learning, and attention-guided synthetic data
generation, aimed to create a balanced training set with
superior synthetic anomalies, ultimately enhancing anomaly
detection accuracy in aircraft equipment. Nevertheless, few
challenges remain in implementation of GAI models. One of
the challenges identified is optimization of the balance
between generator and discriminator for realistic synthetic
data [30,40]. The current research heavily focuses on sensor
data, ignoring the fact that textual and visual data can also
augment GAI models. Furthermore, lack of interpretability
causes lack of trust in the output of GAI models especially
for safety critical systems, as a lesser understanding
generally exists into how these models arrive at their
conclusion. Also, GAI models trained on specific aircraft
system might not work well with other systems, due to sensor
data variations. Finally, the implementation of generative
models in PdM of aircraft is affected by high-dimensional
aircraft data, expensive infrastructure, and real-time
integration challenges. The successful integration of GAI
model in PdM framework depends upon the knowledge
dissemination. The lack of knowledge sharing may result in
a breakdown in communication and make it difficult to
incorporate generative AI models into current operations

[27,30,35].
It was observed that public repositories offer a wealth of
data, with the C-MAPSS emerging as the dominant choice
for turbofan engine research [36,38]. This dataset
encompasses information on 100 engines including run-tofailure data, facilitating RUL prediction studies. However,
C-MAPSS focuses solely on turbofan engines, potentially
limiting research on other critical aircraft components like
bearings and hydraulics.
Despite these limitations, there exists positive prospects
for research in this field. Researchers are trying to explore
various strategies to collect more data through simulation



techniques or collaboration with airlines. Knowledge sharing
between airlines, manufacturers and research institutions can
drastically improve the performance of these models to
predict accurate RUL or degradation pattern. Hence, future
research can be directed towards exploring avenues to collect
extensive data for other aircraft critical systems to accelerate
the development and adoption of GAI for PdM in aviation
industry. Moreover, studies can be undertaken to enable GAI
models to handle multimodal data which is frequently
encountered in real-world scenarios. Research into
integrating these technologies for greater automation can
lead to wider adoption by maintenance personnel and
improved efficiency in anomaly detection processes. This
will ultimately translate to better safety and costeffectiveness for airlines. It is also important to note that the
field of generative AI is not operating in isolation, rather its
combination with other technologies such as digital twins
and sensor technologies create even more comprehensive
models for RUL and fault prediction. While this field is
showing great promises in the aviation industry, researchers
are constantly trying to explore means to improve its
capabilities and increasing the interpretability of generative
models, which will allow for the understanding of its
decision-making process, promoting wider adoption of
generative models in the aviation industry.

**5.** **Limitations of Research**


Although the search protocol was formulated after
thorough testing and refinement; however, it is
acknowledged that the search for relevant papers might have
excluded some valuable studies. Phrasing search queries can
be tricky, and even minor wording changes can significantly
alter search results in different databases. Furthermore, the
search of relevant studies was concluded in Feb 2024,
excluding any research published afterwards. This is a
challenge in a rapidly developing field like generative AI.
With so many new publications, capturing all relevant
literature up to the review's submission date is difficult.
Even with efficient research methods, there's always a time
gap between data collection and the final review. This means
there might be recently published techniques that address the
issues identified in this review, but they weren't included due
to the cutoff date. This can potentially lead to missing
important research on the topic. Another limitation is
publication bias. Studies with positive results are more likely
to be published than those with negative findings. The
authors tried to address this by focusing on high-quality
research using specific methods, but there's still a chance
relevant information was missed. Selection bias is also a
concern. Even with predetermined criteria for including
studies, subjective judgments can still influence which ones
are chosen. The broad scope of the research area,
encompassing both predictive maintenance and generative
AI, requires expertise in various fields. This increases the
possibility of misinterpreting some studies during the


selection process. The data extraction and synthesis process
were equally shared between the authors to capture important
information needed to answer the research questions. The
disagreements were carefully reflected upon, and decisions
were made accordingly. Finally, the study acknowledges
limitations in its internal and external validity. The research
questions themselves might have missed crucial aspects or
made assumptions due to the evolving nature of the field and
the vast number of approaches for developing these
technologies. Moreover, only considering studies that
combined GAI with predictive maintenance limited the
findings' generalizability. This means they might not be
applicable to other forms of predictive maintenance
techniques.

**6.** **Conclusion**


This study explored the growing use of Generative AI in
transforming how the aviation industry performs predictive
maintenance on aircraft. A systematic literature review was
conducted, analyzing studies published over the past five
years. The focus was on understanding how GAI is
revolutionizing aircraft PdM. This included the role
generative models play, the specific models being used, the
areas where GAI is applied, and the datasets employed to
validate these models.
The study implemented a semi-automated approach for
the literature review, utilizing ASReview and PRISMA
techniques. This process identified 16 relevant studies out of
a pool of 442. The findings highlight the significant
contribution of GAI in surpassing traditional PdM methods.
Notably, GAI excels at addressing limitations caused by a
lack of data. This allows for more accurate diagnoses of
equipment degradation and the ability to estimate the RUL
of aircraft systems with greater precision.
The primary objective of utilizing GAI in aircraft PdM
is to generate realistic RUL data. This data is then used to
predict RUL and diagnose faults. GAI proves particularly
adept at tackling challenges like imbalanced and noisy data,
where information on normal operations significantly
outweighs data on faults. GANs, VAE-GANs, and various
combinations of these models are the most prevalent GAI
approaches used for predicting aircraft component RUL.
Across the reviewed studies, NASA's CMAPSS
emerged as the most popular dataset for validating GAIbased PdM models. However, a limitation of this dataset is
its focus on turbofan engines. This restricts the possibilities
for conducting research on PdM for other aircraft
components.
