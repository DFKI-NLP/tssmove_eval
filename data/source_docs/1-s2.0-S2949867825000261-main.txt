**1. Introduction**



A B S T R A C T


Railway safety and efficiency increasingly rely on precise train positioning. The integration of the Global
Navigation Satellite System (GNSS) into railway control systems aims to reduce dependence on track-side in­
frastructure. While GNSS has significantly improved train localization, challenges such as the susceptibility to
jamming remain. To address this, this paper introduces an Inertial Navigation System (INS)-aided train posi­
tioning system based on deep integration, exploring its performance through semi-physical experiments and
simulations. Experimental results demonstrate that the proposed solution is able to reduce the positioning error
by 63.47 %, and the velocity error by 58.47 % under jamming conditions. The study highlights the potential of
deep integration for improving the resilience of GNSS-based train control systems, especially in the face of Radio
Frequency (RF) jamming threats.



Railway safety and efficiency depend increasingly on knowing every
train’s position. Traditional train positioning technologies usually in­
volve track circuit positioning, axle counter positioning, and intersec­
tion inductive loop positioning. However, these methods are often
limited by insufficient positioning accuracy and high maintenance
costs. The application of the Global Navigation Satellite System (GNSS)
in railway train control systems aims to reduce as much as possible the
use of track-side infrastructures for train location awareness, including
track circuits and Balises. GNSS-based train positioning has been ex­
tensively researched worldwide. Specific projects and initiatives have
been undertaken to integrate GNSS into the railway systems.

Early in the 1990s, the United States introduces the concept of the
Positive Train control System (PTS) [1], which relies on Global Posi­
tioning System (GPS), accelerometers, and fiber-optic gyroscopes for
the train positioning function. By December 2020, the United States
achieved 100 % implementation of Positive Train Control (PTC) across
all required railroads, marking a historic milestone in rail safety and
interoperability [2]. CLUG 2.0 project [3] launched in 2023 in Europe
aims to enhance the European Rail Traffic Management System
(ERTMS) by integrating an on-board GNSS+EGNOS-based multi-sensor
fusion architecture. In China, the China State Railway Group Co., Ltd.
introduced a new train control system, the Chinese Train Control



System-New (CTCS-N) [4], which proposed a “train-borne centric” train
operation control method. This system incorporates on-board GNSS
positioning, speed sensors, and electronic railway maps to achieve train
localization. GNSS has been acknowledged as a pivotal enabler in im­
proving the cost efficiency and automation level of the train control
system.

In complex railway operation scenarios, such as urban canyons,
tunnels, and stations with cover, the limited GNSS signal visibility, and
weak observation geometry would greatly decrease the positioning
performance. Thus, the integration solutions are typically proposed for
the train safety positioning. A typical integration solution for GNSSbased train positioning is based on the data fusion with both GNSS and
Inertial Navigation System (INS) sensors. The GNSS/INS integrated
solution is capable of providing full navigation solutions of position,
velocity, and attitude in high positioning accuracy, high updating rate
even under the GNSS limited scenario when the visible satellites are less
than four. Additionally, there is an increasing concern that GNSS is also
vulnerable to radio frequency jamming, due to the weak signal power of
the GNSS signal. More and more GNSS disturbance events are reported
because of jamming, in worldwide, including maritime applications

[5,6], aviation applications [7], and also railway applications [8,9].
GNSS in-band interferences, for example, intentional jamming shows a
source of potential threats to the safe, secure, and resilient operation of
a dense railway system utilizing GNSS as the main positioning method.

It is of great importance to develop specific countermeasures to protect
train positioning from jamming-caused GNSS disturbance.

Research institutions and GNSS receiver manufacturers have been
developing GNSS receivers equipped with anti-jamming capabilities. A
survey of state-of-art approaches is introduced, based on the functional
stages of the receiver in which they operate. Typically, they could be
implemented at the front-end, pre-correction, or post-correlation stages,
with others functioning at the navigation processing stage. Spatial fil­
tering [9–11], functioning at the front-end stage, uses antenna arrays to
point the receiver antenna beam toward the GNSS satellites and away
from jammers. In the pre-correction stage, time/frequency filtering

[12–14] enables the jamming mitigation before the jamming signal
enters the receiver. Vector tracking is a typical approach that works on
the post-correction stage. Instead of tracking in-view satellites sepa­
rately in the conventional baseband signal processing, vector tracking

[15,16] is achieved through feedback of the receiver states from the
navigation module to the signal tracking channels as prior information
and constraints. Additionally, the inertial-aiding-based strategy at the
navigation processing stage provides a different way. INS is a selfcontained navigation approach in which measurements provided by
accelerometers and gyroscopes are used to track the states relative to a
known starting state. A remarkable advantage of an INS is that it is
inherently immune to Radio Frequency (RF) signals, which makes it
possible to benefit from the mitigation of GNSS jamming signals. In the
railway domain, an extensive analysis of interference events based on a
one-year dataset collected across the Swiss railway network was con­
ducted in Ref. [8], which revealed widespread occurrences of GNSS
interference, particularly clustered around larger cities and densely
populated residential areas. A test and evaluation of GNSS-based
railway positioning under various jamming conditions was presented in
Ref. [17], demonstrating the susceptibility of train localization perfor­
mance to interference. A resilient localization framework operating at
the measurement level, incorporating jamming discrimination and
multi-sensor fusion, was proposed in Ref. [18]. Nevertheless, research
on GNSS anti-jamming solutions for railway environments remains re­
latively limited, which motivates the focus of the present work.

GNSS/INS integration can be categorized into three primary archi­
tectures: loosely coupled, tightly coupled, and deeply coupled sys­
tems [19,20]. In a loosely coupled integration, the GNSS receiver and
INS operate independently, with integration occurring at the navigation
solution level by combining the position and velocity outputs from both
systems. This approach requires a minimum of four visible satellites for
a positioning solution, which can be a limitation in environments with
jamming signals. In contrast, tightly-coupled integration integrates raw
GNSS measurements, such as pseudoranges and Doppler shifts, directly
with INS solution within a filter. This method allows the system to
utilize all available satellite signals, even when fewer than four sa­
tellites are visible, enhancing performance in challenging environ­
ments. Deeply coupled, or ultra-tight, integration goes a step further by



incorporating inertial measurements directly into the GNSS signal
tracking loops. This integration aids in maintaining signal lock under
high dynamics and interference conditions, as the INS can provide
predictive information to the GNSS receiver’s tracking algorithms. The
advantages of deep integration are particularly evident in environments
with significant interference or high dynamics. In Ref. [21], the antijamming performance of the INS velocity aiding tracking loop is eval­
uated and compared, using a continuous wave jamming scenario. While
the superiority of INS-aided deep integration has been preliminarily
validated in jamming scenarios, current research on anti-jamming
methods remains constrained by simple jamming models such as con­
tinuous wave jamming. The mechanisms under different jamming
modulation schemes remain underexplored. This study expands the
exploration of jamming types by focusing on sweep-frequency inter­
ference. Considering the compatibility of the GNSS/INS deeply-coupled
integration with the current GNSS-based train positioning function in
the control system conception, this paper proposes an INS-aided train
determination system (LDS) based on the deep integration. The system
profile and the GNSS jamming signals are briefly introduced firstly.
Then, we give a detailed introduction of deeply-coupled integration
design. Finally, a hardware simulator is used and semi-physical ex­
periments are conducted to assess the proposed system’s performance
with a sweep frequency jamming scenario.


**2. System design**


From Fig. 1, it can be found that multiple information resources are
involved in the train positioning system, where the inertial data from
the accelerometers and gyroscopes is taken into the Position, Velocity,
and Timing (PVT) calculation to enhance the resilience to abnormal
jamming attacks. By integrating an INS directly into the train onboard
subsystem, it becomes unnecessary to install or modify the track-side
subsystem, thereby simplifying system design and maintenance,
thereby providing a flexible architecture for modern rail train control
systems.

From a signal processing perspective, jamming signals are collected
and analyzed together with the authentic GNSS signals by the antenna
and the subsequent front-end stage in the GNSS receiver simulta­
neously. The signal contaminated by jamming signals would directly
affect the subsequent stages of the receiver’s processing chain, in­
cluding acquisition and tracking stages. In the tracking stage, jamming
prevents the code and carrier loops from maintaining stable signal
tracking, ultimately resulting in pronounced errors or even failure in
the PVT solution. In the proposed system, the Doppler shift information
derived from INS is fed back to aid the GNSS tracking loop, improving
the overall resilience of the navigation solution. Inertial mechanization
is used to infer Inertial Solution at discrete epochs, while the GNSS
tracking loop operates simultaneously until they meet the integration
epoch. Then, integrated filter realizes the state estimation based on the

**3. Jamming analysis and protection**


_3.1. Jamming analysis_

and falls well below the thermal noise floor. Typically, the received GPS
signal levels fall within the range of −127 dBm [22]. The weak char­

degradation under jamming conditions. Jamming signals may emanate
from telecommunication and electronic systems operating in adjacent

quency Modulation (FM)/TV transmitter harmonics, Amplitude Mod­

The jamming signals will not only disrupt the proper functioning of
the receiver’s RF front-end components, such as low-noise amplifiers,
automatic gain control, and analog-to-digital converters, but also ad­
versely affect the operation of the carrier and code tracking loops. This
jamming can prevent the receiver from acquiring and maintaining a
stable lock on the satellite signals, ultimately leading to degraded po­
sitioning performance.


_3.2. INS-aided jamming protection solution_


Fig. 2 illustrates the INS-aided GNSS tracking loop. The carrier
signal measurement enters the tracking loop through the phase dis­
criminator to generate a phase error estimate. This phase error drives
the carrier loop filter which outputs an estimate of the signal Doppler to
drive the carrier controller oscillator (NCO). The NCO duplicates the
phase of the incoming signal, and this phase estimate is fed back to the
phase discriminator. The tracking loop is usually said to be “locked” if
the phase error is less than five degrees.

[26]. At each GNSS epoch, the Kalman filter is triggered to estimate the
errors in INS-inferred results using the measurement update equations

**4. Results and discussion**


In order to analyze the effectiveness of the jamming protection method
under GNSS jamming conditions, further simulations are carried out in a
signal jamming test environment. The setup of the GNSS jamming test
platform is shown in Fig. 4, consisting of several key components, each
connected through specific signal transmission paths, designed to enable
jamming data collection for analytical processing. The Spirent GSS8000
GNSS simulator and the sight N5172B jamming signal generator are con­
trolled from the simulator’s SimGEN host controller, which generates the
required GPS L1 RF signals and the jamming signals, respectively. The
jamming signals are defined, along with all the other scenario parameters,
within the SimGEN. The scenario trajectory collected in the field is input
into the SimGEN software, driving the simulator to generate the corre­
sponding dynamic GNSS RF signals. A signal combiner is utilized to in­
tegrate the clean GPS RF signal and the specific jamming signal. Since the
down-converted Intermediate Frequency (IF) signal is what we need, the
HG-softGPS06 GNSS IF signal collector is connected to the simulated signal
to capture the necessary data, which is then collected and post-processed by
the PC host. Field test results from a train location system were collected
and used as the scenario script to drive the controller. A tactical-grade SPAN
system (a high-precision GNSS/INS integrated system with a tactical grade,
low noise IMU-FSAS) installed on the train was utilized to collect the tra­
jectory and the IMU data.

Two practical local trajectories that last for 160 s are adopted as the
operation script to generate different GNSS jamming tested scenarios
and drive the GNSS simulator. The key parameters of the trajectory are
listed as follows.

In the simulated test, a GNSS signal at GPS L1 frequency is simulated
with a reference level of −130 dBm, while the jamming generator
operates at a center frequency of 1575.42 MHz with a power level of
−90 dBm. The Jamming-to-Signal Ratio (JSR), i.e., the ratio of the
jamming signal power to the GNSS signal power, is used to express the
relationship between GNSS signal power and jamming power. Beyond
complete signal obstruction scenarios, intermediate jamming power
levels can induce significant performance degradation in GNSS re­
ceivers without fully blocking satellite signal acquisition. These inter­
mediate power levels would be hazardous and difficult to detect and
mitigate, making it crucial to accurately assess the impact of jamming
on GNSS signal quality. Previous research [18] has demonstrated sub­
stantial signal quality degradation under jamming conditions with JSR
values ranging from 38 to 54 dB. Accordingly, this study focuses on a
40 dB JSR scenario, corresponding to the specified −130 dBm GPS
signal and −90 dBm jamming power configuration.



A 5 Hz ramp waveform is employed, sweeping ± 1 kHz around the
carrier. The jamming signal for investigating the collected IF signal, and
the software-defined radio GNSS receiver are used to analyze the data.
This software receiver is configured with a second-order phase-locked
loop and a second-order code delay lock loop. The carrier loop band­
width is set to 3 Hz, while the code loop bandwidth is maintained at
3 Hz. In the proposed solution, INS mechanization updates every 1 ms,
while the tracking loop coherent integration time is also set to 1 ms.

**5. Conclusion**


In this paper, a GNSS jamming protection solution is presented
based on the INS-aided deep integration solution. The solution enables
a self-contained PVT reference that is immune to RF signals. INS in­
formation is sent to the receiver’s baseband, realizing the assistance to
the receiver at the signal processing level. The measured velocity in­
formation from INS is mapped to the LOS between the satellite and the
vehicle to obtain the Doppler of relative motion. The Doppler is fed
back to the NCO in the tracking loop to aid the tracking process. The
evaluation of the proposed solution demonstrates significant



improvements in positioning and velocity, enabling resilient train po­
sitioning for securing next generation GNSS-based train control sys­
tems. Future work will focus on refining mitigation techniques with
extended studies for multi-constellation scenarios (e.g., BeiDou and
Galileo), and exploring field tests in real lines to ensure the resilience of
GNSS-based train localization systems in railway applications.
